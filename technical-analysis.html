Technical Analysis

Master traditional chart patterns, indicators, and price action techniques that professional traders use to identify market opportunities.

The Art and Science of Reading Charts

Technical analysis is the study of historical price data and volume to forecast future price movements. Unlike fundamental analysis, which examines financial statements and economic factors, technical analysis focuses solely on the price action in the markets. Our Technical Analysis program teaches students how to interpret charts, identify patterns, and use technical indicators to make more informed trading decisions.

Chart Types and Time Frames

Understanding different chart types and selecting appropriate time frames is essential for effective technical analysis. Our program covers:

  • Line Charts - Simple representations of closing prices that help identify overall trends.
  • Bar Charts - Displaying open, high, low, and close prices to show more detailed price action.
  • Candlestick Charts - Japanese charting method that provides rich visual information about market psychology.
  • Point and Figure Charts - Focus on significant price movements while filtering out minor fluctuations.
  • Time Frame Selection - Choosing appropriate intervals from tick charts to monthly charts based on trading style and goals.

Example: Reading a Candlestick

Candlestick charts convey multiple pieces of information in a single price bar:

- A green (or white) candle indicates the close was higher than the open

- A red (or black) candle indicates the close was lower than the open

- The "body" shows the range between open and close prices

- The "wicks" or "shadows" show the high and low prices

Specific candlestick patterns like "Doji," "Hammer," or "Engulfing Pattern" can signal potential reversals or continuation of trends.

Trend Analysis

Identifying and following trends is one of the most fundamental aspects of technical analysis. We explore:

  • Trend Identification - Recognizing uptrends, downtrends, and sideways markets.
  • Support and Resistance - Identifying price levels where buying or selling pressure typically intensifies.
  • Trendlines - Drawing and using trendlines to visualize market direction and potential reversals.
  • Channels - Trading within price channels to capitalize on range-bound markets.
  • Fibonacci Retracements - Using mathematical ratios to identify potential reversal levels within a trend.

Chart Patterns

Chart patterns are recurring formations that appear on price charts and can indicate future price movements. Our program covers:

  • Continuation Patterns - Formations like flags, pennants, and triangles that suggest the prevailing trend will resume.
  • Reversal Patterns - Formations such as head and shoulders, double tops/bottoms, and rounding patterns that signal potential trend changes.
  • Volume Patterns - How trading volume can confirm or contradict price patterns.
  • Candlestick Patterns - Multi-candle formations that provide insights into market psychology and potential price movements.
  • Harmonic Patterns - Complex geometric price patterns based on Fibonacci ratios.

Technical Indicators

Technical indicators are mathematical calculations based on price, volume, or open interest that help traders analyze markets. We examine:

  1. Trend Indicators - Moving averages, MACD, Parabolic SAR, and ADX for identifying and confirming trends.
  2. Momentum Indicators - RSI, Stochastic Oscillator, and CCI for measuring the speed of price movements.
  3. Volatility Indicators - Bollinger Bands, ATR, and Keltner Channels for measuring market volatility.
  4. Volume Indicators - On-Balance Volume, Volume Profile, and Money Flow Index for analyzing trading volume.
  5. Custom Indicators - Creating and optimizing personalized indicators for specific trading approaches.

Example: Using the RSI Indicator

The Relative Strength Index (RSI) is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and change of price movements on a scale from 0 to 100:

- RSI above 70 is traditionally considered overbought (potential sell signal)

- RSI below 30 is traditionally considered oversold (potential buy signal)

- Divergence between RSI and price can signal potential reversals

- In strong trends, RSI can remain in overbought/oversold territory for extended periods

Rather than using RSI in isolation, experienced traders combine it with trend analysis and other confirmation signals.

Price Action Trading

Price action trading focuses on the movement of price itself without relying heavily on indicators. Our program explores:

  • Reading individual candlesticks and bar patterns
  • Identifying and trading key support/resistance levels
  • Recognizing supply and demand zones
  • Using multiple time frame analysis
  • Understanding market structure with higher highs/lows and lower highs/lows
