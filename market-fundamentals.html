Market Fundamentals

Learn the basics of financial markets, including different asset classes, market structure, and fundamental concepts that drive price movements.

Understanding Financial Markets

The Market Fundamentals program is designed to give students a strong foundation in the core concepts that underpin global financial markets. Whether you're completely new to investing or have some experience, this program will help you understand how markets function, the different types of assets available to traders, and the key factors that influence market movements.

Asset Classes Explained

Financial markets are organized around different types of assets, each with unique characteristics, risk profiles, and trading mechanics. Our program covers the major asset classes in detail:

  • Equities (Stocks) - Ownership stakes in public companies that represent a claim on future earnings and assets.
  • Fixed Income (Bonds) - Debt instruments that provide regular interest payments and return of principal at maturity.
  • Currencies (Forex) - The global market for exchanging national currencies, the largest and most liquid market in the world.
  • Commodities - Physical goods such as gold, oil, agricultural products, and other raw materials.
  • Derivatives - Financial contracts whose value is derived from underlying assets (options, futures, swaps).
  • Cryptocurrencies - Digital or virtual currencies that use cryptography for security and operate independently of central banks.

Example: Market Capitalization

Market capitalization (or "market cap") is the total dollar value of a company's outstanding shares of stock. It's calculated by multiplying the current share price by the total number of outstanding shares.

For example, if a company has 10 million shares outstanding and the current share price is $50, the market cap would be:

Market Cap = 10,000,000 shares × $50 = $500,000,000 (or $500 million)

Companies are often categorized by market cap as large-cap ($10+ billion), mid-cap ($2-10 billion), or small-cap (under $2 billion).

Market Structure and Participants

Financial markets involve many different participants, each with their own objectives, strategies, and impact on market dynamics. Our program examines the roles of:

  • Exchanges - Organized marketplaces where securities, commodities, derivatives, and other financial instruments are traded.
  • Brokers and Dealers - Intermediaries who facilitate transactions between buyers and sellers.
  • Market Makers - Entities that stand ready to buy and sell a particular security on a regular and continuous basis.
  • Institutional Investors - Large organizations (pension funds, mutual funds, insurance companies) that manage substantial portfolios.
  • Retail Investors - Individual investors who buy and sell securities for personal accounts.
  • Regulators - Government agencies that oversee markets and enforce rules to ensure fairness and stability.

Fundamental Factors That Drive Markets

Understanding what moves markets is essential for any trader. Our program covers the key drivers of market movements:

  1. Economic Indicators - GDP, employment data, inflation, and other metrics that reflect the health of economies.
  2. Monetary Policy - Central bank actions regarding interest rates and money supply that influence borrowing, spending, and investment.
  3. Corporate Earnings - Company performance reports that impact stock valuations and investor sentiment.
  4. Geopolitical Events - Political developments, conflicts, treaties, and other global events that affect market stability and confidence.
  5. Sector Trends - Industry-specific developments and innovations that drive investment in particular market segments.
  6. Market Sentiment - Collective attitudes and emotions of investors that can drive market momentum.

The Basics of Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis involves evaluating securities by examining related economic, financial, and other qualitative and quantitative factors. Our program introduces students to:

  • Reading and interpreting financial statements
  • Key valuation metrics (P/E ratio, EPS, dividend yield, etc.)
  • Industry analysis and competitive positioning
  • Economic analysis and its impact on sectors and companies
